Acknowledgement of Country

Understanding and practising respectful acknowledgement of Traditional Custodians.

What is an Acknowledgement of Country?

An Acknowledgement of Country is a formal statement recognising the Traditional Custodians of the land where an event or gathering takes place. It acknowledges their connection to the land, waters, and community, and the importance of that connection.

Anyone can deliver an Acknowledgement of Country - you do not need to be an Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander person to do so.

Acknowledgement vs Welcome to Country

These are two distinct practices and it is important not to confuse them:

Acknowledgement of Country
  • Can be delivered by anyone
  • Is a sign of respect and recognition for the Traditional Custodians
  • Common at events, meetings, and other gatherings
Welcome to Country
  • Can ONLY be performed by a Traditional Custodian or Elder
  • A formal ceremony welcoming visitors to their Country
  • Requires coordination with local Elders

Traditional Custodians of Australian Capital Cities

  • Brisbane - Turrbal and Jagera peoples
  • Sydney - Gadigal of the Eora Nation
  • Melbourne - Wurundjeri Woi-wurrung and Bunurong / Boon Wurrung peoples of the Kulin
  • Canberra - Ngunnawal people
  • Adelaide - Kaurna people
  • Perth - Whadjuk Nyoongar people
  • Darwin - Larrakia people
  • Hobart - Palawa people

Finding Traditional Custodians of a Specific Place

If your event is outside of a capital city, here are reliable ways to find the Traditional Custodians:

  • Visit your local government website - most councils acknowledge Traditional Custodians in their area
  • Contact your local Land Council representing Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander communities
  • Use the AIATSIS Map of Indigenous Australia - a detailed map showing the many groups and their boundaries
  • Visit Know Your Country for help identifying the First Nations Country you are on

Common Mistakes and Contested Issues

  • Being tokenistic - rushing through it without genuine meaning or understanding
  • Wrong Country name - using an incorrect or generalised group name for the location
  • Confusing Acknowledgement with Welcome to Country - they are not interchangeable
  • Treating it as a formality - reading words off a page without personal connection or thought
  • Generic statements everywhere - using the same statement regardless of location
